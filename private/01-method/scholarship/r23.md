# R23 — Dative Subject of Infinitive Scholarship

Substrate for canon §3.12 (R23 Dative Indirect Object as Semantic Subject of Infinitive). The operational rule lives in the canon entry; this document carries the grammatical-grounding citations and the discrepancy with classical-Greek-grammar terminology.

## Why our usage diverges from standard grammar terminology

Standard Greek-grammar reference works reserve "subject of the infinitive" for the **accusative case**, per:

- **BDF §392** — accusative-subject of the infinitive in indirect discourse / object infinitives.
- **Wallace §195** (and ch. on infinitive) — the accusative-subject-of-infinitive default.
- **Burton §390** — accusative as the formally-named subject of the infinitive.

In our colometric usage, the **dative** in constructions like Rom 12:3 (`Λέγω ... παντὶ τῷ ὄντι ἐν ὑμῖν μὴ ὑπερφρονεῖν`) is grammatically the indirect object of the speech verb (`Λέγω`) — but it is ALSO coreferential with the understood subject of the infinitive (`ὑπερφρονεῖν`). The dative is the participant who is told *and* the participant who is to do (or refrain from doing) the action named by the infinitive.

For colometric purposes, this dative chunks with the **infinitive content**, not with the speech-verb frame. The line-break belongs between the speech frame (`Λέγω γὰρ διὰ τῆς χάριτος τῆς δοθείσης μοι`) and the addressee + content unit (`παντὶ τῷ ὄντι ἐν ὑμῖν μὴ ὑπερφρονεῖν παρ' ὃ δεῖ φρονεῖν,`). Splitting the dative away from its semantically-controlled infinitive fragments the propositional unit.

## Colometric analogue to accusative-subject-of-infinitive

The same colometric reasoning applies to accusative-subject constructions in indirect discourse — the accusative-subject chunks with the infinitive content. The dative case here is structurally analogous despite the grammatical-case difference: both mark the participant whose action is named by the infinitive, and both belong with the infinitive in the line-break layout.

## Why the rule is named "Dative Indirect Object as Semantic Subject" rather than "Dative-Subject Infinitive"

We do not claim the dative is grammatically the subject of the infinitive (that would be a terminological move that contradicts BDF / Wallace / Burton). The rule names the **colometric function** the dative serves — being the semantic agent of the infinitive — without rewriting the grammatical case-assignment.
