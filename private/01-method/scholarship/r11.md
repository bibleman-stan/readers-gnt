# R11 — Speech-Introduction Scholarly Substrate

Operational content for R11 lives in canon §3.6. This document carries the canonical-cases evidence (verse references corroborating the closed-list and exclusion semantics) and the structural-reasoning narratives that the rule-template forbids inline.

## OT-Attribution tags inside quotation blocks (Exclusion 1)

A speech verb appearing INSIDE an already-opened quotation block as an attribution marker (mid-quote, post-first-word, flanked by commas, or appearing after content) is NOT a fresh speech-introduction. The R11 detector treats these as merged with surrounding quoted content.

Canonical cases:

- **Revelation 2–3 letter-closings** — `λέγει τὸ πνεῦμα ταῖς ἐκκλησίαις` repeated ×7 in the seven-letter sequence. Each is an OT-style attribution tag within a prophetic letter's quoted content, not a fresh speech-intro.
- **Pauline OT citations** — Rom 12:19 `λέγει κύριος`; Rom 14:11 `λέγει κύριος`; 1 Cor 14:21 `λέγει κύριος`; 2 Cor 6:17 `λέγει κύριος`. The quoted OT passage already has its speech-intro upstream; the embedded `λέγει κύριος` is attribution-tag, not intro.
- **Hebrews** — Heb 8:5, 8:8–9 `λέγει κύριος` / `φησίν` inside OT quotation blocks.
- **Acts** — Acts 7:7 `λέγει ὁ θεός`; 7:49 `λέγει κύριος` inside Stephen's quoted-OT narration.

Rationale: these are attribution markers serving the same function as "he said" parenthetically interjected in English dialogue. The canon treats them as continuation of the quoted content, not as re-introducing speech.

## Parenthetical mid-speech attribution (Exclusion 2)

A speech verb (typically `φησίν`, occasionally `λέγει`/`εἶπεν`) appearing as a parenthetical interjection inside a single-sentence quoted speech — flanked by commas on both sides, mid-line position, with surrounding tokens being quoted content — stays merged. This is the same merge-discipline as OT-attribution, applied outside extended quotation blocks.

Canonical cases:

- **Matt 14:8** — `Δός μοι, φησίν, ὧδε ἐπὶ πίνακι τὴν κεφαλὴν Ἰωάννου τοῦ βαπτιστοῦ.` — "'Give me,' she says, 'the head of John the Baptist here on a platter.'" The `φησίν` is an attribution interjection; splitting it out would mechanize a natural mid-sentence pause and dilute the quoted speech.
- **Acts 25:22** — `Αὔριον, φησίν, ἀκούσῃ αὐτοῦ.` — "'Tomorrow,' he says, 'you will hear him.'"

Rationale: parenthetical attribution is a prose convention, not a new speech event. Treating it as a speech-intro would generate three-line micro-fragments where the original text reads as a single flowing quotation.

Contrast with R11 genuine fire: a speech-intro that OPENS new speech appears at line-start, immediately followed by ano teleia (`·`) or colon, then quoted content on the NEXT line. If the speech verb is mid-line between quoted content, it is NOT a new speech-intro — it is parenthetical attribution.

## ἀπεκρίθη + λέγων Semitic idiom (Exclusion 4)

The `ἀπεκρίθη ... λέγων·` double-verb construction is a Semitic idiom in which `λέγων` is a redundant manner marker, not a second speech introduction. The entire construction collapses onto one line under R11. R28-ext does not fire because `λέγων` is participial (non-finite), not a standalone finite speech-intro.

Canonical cases: Matt 11:25, Matt 12:38, Mark 9:5, Luke 17:17, and parallel pericopes throughout the Synoptics and Acts. The pattern is sufficiently uniform that the R11 detector's exclusion fires on the surface pattern (finite-aorist `ἀπεκρίθη`/`ἀποκριθείς` + participial `λέγων` on same line) without requiring additional semantic disambiguation.

## Synonymous-doublet imperatives (Exclusion 5)

Two imperatives uttered as a single rebuke with one breath — where the second is an intensifying restatement rather than a distinct action — merge onto one line.

Canonical case: **Mark 4:39** `Σιώπα, πεφίμωσο.` — "Be silent! Be muzzled!" The second imperative does not command a new action; it intensifies the first.

Test: if the second imperative is a synonym or intensifier with no new content, merge. If it commands a distinct action, split. The test is editorial-judgment; the detector surfaces candidates only.

## Amen-formula speech-intro reasoning

The solemnity-prefixed formula `ἀμήν [ἀμήν] λέγω σοι/ὑμῖν` is structurally a complete speech-intro unit. R11's general rule (speech-intro own line) applies, but the amen-prefix makes the formula structurally unambiguous in a way bare `λέγω ὑμῖν` is not — bare `λέγω ὑμῖν` can merge under some contexts (speech-frame aggregation, parenthetical attribution), but the amen-prefix forecloses those alternative readings by marking solemnity-pronouncement.

Form variants:
- **Single-amen** — Synoptic Gospels (Matt/Mark/Luke). `ἀμὴν λέγω ὑμῖν,` + content next line.
- **Doubled-amen** — Johannine corpus. `ἀμὴν ἀμὴν λέγω σοι·` + content next line.

Both follow the same structural convention; the doubling is a Johannine stylistic marker, not a structural difference.

## Repeated-vocative carve-out

R11 does NOT govern repeated-vocative address (e.g., `Κύριε κύριε` — Matt 7:21–22, Luke 6:46). The vocative pair is a single speech act rhetorical unit under R7/§3.9 vocative-indivisibility and stays merged on one line. See R7 / §3.9 for vocative scope.

## Speech-intro frame aggregation (Exclusion 6 boundary)

The speech-introducing apparatus may merge with a preceding qualifying clause *only when the qualifier is structurally minimal* — a bare ἐάν/ὅταν + subjunctive with no substantial finite-clause content of its own (e.g., `ἐὰν μὴ λέγω·`). When the qualifier is substantive — it contains a finite verb, object NPs, and scene-setting content independent of the speech verb — it is NOT a mere qualifier; it is a full adverbial clause, and **R28-ext** mandates the split. The scope boundary applies cleanly: structurally trivial qualifiers aggregate; anything with a full finite-clause body (subject, verb, object) falls under R28-ext.
